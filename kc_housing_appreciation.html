Price-Tier Convergence and Spatial Housing Appreciation: Evidence from the Kansas City MSA

Matthew Ong · 2021–2026 · Cross-Sectional OLS Analysis

SAS QGIS R Zillow ZHVI
Code Repository github.com/MatthewOng01/KC_House_Price_Appreciation — SAS →

Overview

This project analyzes five-year cumulative home price appreciation across 198 ZIP codes in the Kansas City Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA) to evaluate whether spatial centrality or baseline price tier better explains metropolitan housing returns.

Lower-priced ZIP codes appreciated proportionally faster over 2021–2026 — consistent with β-convergence dynamics. Proximity to the historic urban core has negligible practical impact once starting price tier is accounted for.

Using Zillow Home Value Index (ZHVI) data and cross-sectional OLS models with interaction effects, the analysis finds strong evidence of price-tier convergence. The preferred specification explains approximately 35% of cross-sectional variation in five-year appreciation (R² ≈ 0.34), with results carrying direct implications for real estate underwriting, capital allocation, and urban infrastructure planning.

Methodology

1. Price Appreciation Metric

Five-year cumulative price appreciation is measured using log price change. This formulation reduces skewness in housing price distributions while permitting approximate percentage interpretation:

Price Appreciation = ln(Pfinal) − ln(Pinitial)

2. Spatial Variables

Distances were computed in QGIS from ZIP-code centroids to five major Points of Interest (POIs), then exported as CSV inputs for regression analysis:

dist_plazaThe Plaza
dist_powerlightPower & Light District
dist_legendsThe Legends
dist_mciMCI Airport
dist_lees_summitLee’s Summit

An urban core distance composite was constructed as the mean of the two primary commercial hubs, capturing compounded centrality relative to Kansas City’s historic commercial core:

UrbanCoreDist = (DistancePlaza + DistancePowerLight) / 2

3. Econometric Specification

Three OLS specifications are estimated in sequence, adding complexity to isolate the relative contributions of spatial and price-tier effects:

Baseline Appreciationi = β0 + β1 Distancei + εi
Extended Adds UrbanCoreDist composite as the primary spatial regressor
Interaction (Preferred) UrbanCoreDist × ln(Pinitial) — tests whether the distance effect varies across price tiers

All specifications include diagnostic checks for heteroskedasticity and multicollinearity.

Key Findings

1

Stronger suburban appreciation. Peripheral ZIP codes exhibit stronger five-year price appreciation relative to the urban core, consistent with outward migration and shifting demand patterns over 2021–2026.

2

Price tier is the primary driver. Baseline price segmentation — not urban centrality — accounts for most observed growth patterns. Lower-priced ZIP codes appreciated faster, consistent with β-convergence dynamics.

3

Interaction term: significant but economically small. The interaction model confirms that the distance effect varies across price tiers, but the magnitude is modest — reinforcing that proportional catch-up dynamics, not location, explain the dominant pattern.

4

Preferred model R² ≈ 0.34. The interaction specification explains approximately 35% of cross-sectional variation in five-year appreciation — a strong result given the parsimony of the spatial regressors.

Investment & Planning Implications

The convergence result — that lower-priced ZIP codes appreciated faster over the study period — carries distinct implications depending on stakeholder perspective.

Developers & Asset Managers

Peripheral ZIP codes may present under-recognized appreciation opportunities. Spatial econometric evidence of location-driven capital appreciation can sharpen underwriting models and inform where to allocate development capital ahead of demand trends.

Urban Planners & Local Government

Rapid outer-rim appreciation may warrant strategic attention to infrastructure, zoning, and service delivery. Price catch-up in lower-tier submarkets can signal emerging affordability pressures as appreciation outpaces income growth.

Market Analysts

Cross-sectional appreciation models should center price-tier controls as a primary covariate. Distance from the urban core, while intuitive, carries limited explanatory power once initial price levels are accounted for.
