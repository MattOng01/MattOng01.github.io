Macroeconomics · Policy Analysis

The Use of Macroprudential Policies to Combat Macro Fluctuations

Matthew Ong · 2023 · University of Dallas

Macroeconomics Financial Economics Central Banking Economic Theory

Overview

Our response to macroeconomic crises is constrained by the tools available and the speed at which they can be deployed. This paper explores those limitations and the emergence of a new class of policy instruments — macroprudential policies — that have developed in the years since the 2008 Financial Crisis.

Macroprudential policy seeks to maintain adequate liquidity in the economy during all stages of the business cycle, empowering policymakers to direct liquidity or alter regulations during emergencies with far greater speed and precision than traditional monetary or fiscal tools allow.

The paper traces these tools through two recent stress tests: their deployment during the COVID-19 recession and during the banking crisis of March 2023, when Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank failed in rapid succession. It also addresses a key limitation of quantitative research in this area — because implementation details are withheld for weeks or months to prevent shocks to consumer confidence, rigorous empirical analysis of specific policy actions remains difficult.

Presented at the 2023 Southwestern Social Sciences Association Conference — Fort Worth, TX

Core Argument

What Are Macroprudential Policies?

Unlike monetary policy, which operates broadly through interest rates and money supply, or microprudential policy, which targets individual firms, macroprudential tools occupy a middle ground — system-wide in scope but more targeted in application. They fall into two categories:

Structural Policies Pre-emptive tools that reduce risk buildup before instability occurs — capital requirements, reserve ratios, countercyclical capital buffers, and portfolio-risk limits for systemically important banks.
Cyclical Policies Emergency tools activated during crises to ensure liquidity flows to all parts of the economy — central bank lending facilities, IMF credit lines, and currency swap arrangements between central banks.

The key advantage over traditional tools is adaptability: macroprudential policies can be deployed rapidly, calibrated to the specific nature of a shock, and adjusted or withdrawn as conditions change — without the implementation lags associated with fiscal policy or the blunt impact of broad monetary tightening.

Dollar Appreciation and the Plaza Accord Parallel

The paper draws a direct comparison between two episodes of extreme Dollar appreciation: the 1980–1985 period, when the Dollar rose nearly 50% against major currencies following Volcker-era rate hikes, and the 2022 episode, when the Dollar appreciated over 20% against the Euro during the Fed's post-COVID tightening cycle. In 1985, the only available response was the Plaza Accord — an unprecedented act of coordinated currency intervention by the G-5 nations. The paper argues that modern macroprudential tools make such drastic measures unnecessary by providing more targeted stabilization mechanisms before exchange rate distortions reach crisis levels.

The Basel Accords and Banking Regulation

The paper traces the evolution of international banking standards through three rounds of Basel Accords — each prompted by macroeconomic instability — culminating in Basel III's comprehensive capital, liquidity, and countercyclical buffer requirements following the 2008 Financial Crisis. This framework represents the first true global coordination of structural macroprudential policy.

Case Studies

COVID-19 Response

The pandemic triggered rapid deployment of both cyclical and structural macroprudential tools globally. The IMF opened new targeted lending facilities — including the Rapid Credit Facility in October 2022 — to support low-income countries facing simultaneous inflation and balance-of-payments crises. The Federal Reserve expanded its existing lending infrastructure and coordinated Dollar swap lines with major central banks to ensure global Dollar liquidity.

Silicon Valley Bank and the 2023 Banking Crisis

On March 10, 2023, the FDIC took control of Silicon Valley Bank — the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history — after a $42 billion single-day withdrawal attempt. Two days later, Signature Bank also failed. The Federal Reserve responded by establishing the Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP), offering one-year loans collateralized at par value against Treasuries and agency securities, directly addressing the $1.7 trillion in unrealized losses held by U.S. banks at end of 2022. Crucially, the Fed raised interest rates a further 25 basis points just twelve days later — a move made possible by confidence that macroprudential tools had contained systemic risk.

Key Takeaways

1

Macroprudential tools are more flexible and faster to deploy than fiscal or monetary policy, making them particularly effective at containing acute financial stress without disrupting the broader policy stance.

2

The 2023 banking crisis response — establishing the BTFP while continuing monetary tightening — would not have been possible in 1985 or 2008, illustrating how the macroprudential toolkit has materially expanded policymakers' options.

3

Macroprudential policy works best in coordination with monetary and fiscal tools, not as a replacement. The three form an interlocking whole that requires some degree of policy coordination to be effective.

4

A significant research gap remains: because implementation details are withheld to protect consumer confidence, rigorous quantitative evaluation of specific macroprudential actions is limited and warrants further study as data becomes available.
