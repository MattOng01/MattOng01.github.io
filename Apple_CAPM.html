CAPM: Estimating Apple's Market Sensitivity Against the S&P 500

Matthew Ong · Oct 2020–Oct 2025 · Daily Log Returns

Financial Economics CAPM AAPL R

Overview

This project applies the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to estimate the relationship between Apple Inc.'s returns and overall market movements represented by the S&P 500 Index from October 2020 to October 2025. CAPM provides a simple but powerful framework for understanding how investors are compensated for taking on additional risk beyond the risk-free rate.

Apple's beta of 1.26 indicates its returns tend to move about 26% more than the market. The model explained 57% of Apple's daily excess returns — strong for a single-factor regression.

Using daily log returns and an OLS regression, the analysis estimates Apple's systematic risk exposure and compares the model's predicted annual return (11.12%) against the realized return (11.92%). The project also explores two practical applications of CAPM: portfolio construction and event-study analysis in legal contexts.

Methodology

Data

Daily closing prices for Apple (AAPL) and the S&P 500 Index were obtained from NASDAQ for the period October 1, 2020 to October 1, 2025. Each daily price was converted to a log return to normalize scale and make cross-asset comparisons interpretable:

Rt = ln(Pt / Pt−1)

The risk-free rate is proxied by U.S. Treasury Bills, annualized at 4.5% and converted to a daily rate using 252 trading days as the industry convention:

RF,daily = ln((1 + 0.045)1/252)

Excess returns are then computed for both assets as the difference between each asset's daily return and the daily risk-free rate.

Model Specification

CAPM is estimated via OLS regression of Apple's excess returns on the market's excess returns:

rApple − rF = α + βApple × (rS&P 500 − rF) + ε

The beta coefficient (β) captures Apple's sensitivity to systematic market risk. A value above 1 indicates the stock tends to amplify market movements in both directions. The alpha (α) captures any return not explained by market exposure — a statistically significant positive alpha would indicate consistent outperformance.

Predicted vs. Realized Return

To validate the regression results, the model's annualized predicted return was compared against Apple's realized return over the study period. Daily log returns were annualized and converted from continuously compounded to simple returns:

RAnnual = eRAnnual,log − 1

The CAPM-predicted return of 11.12% closely tracks the realized return of 11.92%, yielding an abnormal return of just +0.80% — consistent with the statistically insignificant alpha and confirming that CAPM captures Apple's risk-return profile well over this period.

Implications

Portfolio Construction

An investor seeking exposure beyond the risk-free rate or broad index returns can use CAPM to benchmark individual stocks by their systematic risk. Apple's beta of 1.26 indicates above-market volatility, making it a suitable holding for investors willing to accept higher short-term swings in exchange for higher expected returns. Repeating this analysis across a set of candidate stocks enables efficient portfolio construction by balancing expected return against each asset's market sensitivity.

Event Study & Legal Analysis

CAPM has a less widely known but important application in legal contexts. Suppose a whistleblower released false information about Apple on October 2, 2025, causing the stock to drop 15%. If the S&P 500 had risen over the same period while Apple's price remained suppressed, CAPM provides a principled method to isolate how much of Apple's decline was attributable to the false report versus general market conditions. The abnormal return — the gap between predicted and realized performance — becomes the basis for estimating economic damages in a defamation or securities fraud lawsuit.

Limitations

Model Assumptions CAPM assumes a single-period planning horizon and identical investor expectations — conditions that rarely hold in practice.
Single Factor Only market covariance is used to capture risk. Richer models such as Fama–French (1993) or Carhart (1997) incorporate additional risk factors for potentially more accurate predictions.
Autocorrelation OLS assumes independent residuals over time. Investor momentum, news cycles, and seasonal patterns can violate this assumption in daily return data.
Random Walk Fama's Efficient Market Hypothesis implies returns should follow a random walk. Empirical deviations — momentum, mean-reversion — suggest OLS may not be the ideal estimator for this setting.
