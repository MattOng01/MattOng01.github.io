Overview

This project replicates and extends the 2015 inflation forecasting model associated with Janet Yellen, evaluating its performance during the COVID-era inflation period from 2020 to 2023.

The central question is whether standard pre-pandemic predictors of inflation — labor market tightness, inflation expectations, and import pressure — remain adequate during a period of acute supply-side disruption, or whether direct measures of supply chain congestion add meaningful explanatory power.

The Global Supply Chain Pressure Index outperforms the Import Price Index as a predictor of COVID-era inflation, while port-level congestion data prove statistically inconclusive due to limited observations.

In addition to replicating the original specification, the project compares alternative supply chain indices and introduces direct port congestion data obtained via request from the U.S. Department of Transportation — data not typically available in standard macroeconomic datasets.

Research Question

Did import pressure and supply chain congestion significantly contribute to U.S. inflation during the COVID period? The analysis addresses two specific sub-questions:

Supply Chain Index Does the Global Supply Chain Pressure Index (GSCPI) outperform the Import Price Index (IPI) as a predictor of inflation?
Port-Level Data Do observed port wait times and cargo volume provide additional explanatory power beyond aggregate supply chain indices?

Data

All variables are converted to quarterly percent changes. The baseline replication spans 1983–2023; the port congestion extension is limited to 2018–2022 by data availability, yielding as few as 12–15 observations in models that include port-level variables.

Model Specification

Four OLS specifications are estimated, each adding complexity to isolate the contribution of supply-side variables to inflation during the pandemic period.

Baseline — Yellen (2015) Specification

CPIt = β0 + β1CPIt−1 + β2CPIt−2 + β3EIt + β4(U/V)t + β5IPIt + εt

Alternative — CEA-Style Modification

CPIt = β0 + β1CPIt−1 + β2CPIt−2 + β3EIt + β4(U/V)t + β5GSCPIt + εt

Replaces IPI with the GSCPI to test whether a broader supply chain index improves fit.

Extended Models — Port-Level Data

Port Wait Time Baseline specification + β6WaitTimet
Cargo Volume Baseline specification + β6Volumet

All models use OLS with a quarterly lag structure to address autocorrelation. Estimation method is lm() in R, with output formatted via stargazer.

Key Findings

1

GSCPI outperforms IPI. The Global Supply Chain Pressure Index is statistically significant and economically meaningful as a predictor of COVID-era inflation, outperforming the Import Price Index in explanatory power.

2

IPI shows weaker fit. The Import Price Index, used in the original Yellen specification, captures less of the inflation variation during the pandemic period relative to the broader GSCPI measure.

3

Port data: directionally consistent, statistically inconclusive. Port wait time and cargo volume carry coefficient signs consistent with supply-side inflation theory, but neither achieves statistical significance — a result attributable largely to the limited sample of 12–15 observations.

4

Supply-side channels matter. Taken together, results suggest that aggregate supply chain pressure indices meaningfully improve inflation forecasting during the COVID period, supporting a supply-side interpretation of the 2021–2023 inflation surge.

Limitations

Results should be interpreted as exploratory rather than causal. Several constraints bear on the analysis:

Sample Size Port-level regressions have only 12–15 observations, substantially limiting statistical power.
Endogeneity Potential endogeneity between supply chain pressure and inflation is not formally addressed.
Structural Break The COVID period may represent a structural break, affecting coefficient stability across the full 1983–2023 sample.
Causal Inference OLS time-series results establish association but cannot establish causal direction without further identification strategy.
