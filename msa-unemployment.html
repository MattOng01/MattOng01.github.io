GDP Growth and Racial Unemployment Gaps Across U.S. Metro Areas

Matthew Ong· April 2026· SAS · BEA · U.S. Census Bureau

Code Repository github.com/MatthewOng01/msa_unemployment →

Overview

Does a metro area's economic growth translate into more equitable labor market outcomes for minority workers? This project takes a closer look at that question by analyzing GDP growth trajectories and racial unemployment gaps across U.S. Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSAs) from 2010 to 2023.

Rather than ranking metros by raw growth alone, I constructed a composite performance score that rewards consistent, stable growth over volatile boom-and-bust cycles. The top and bottom ten metros by this score were then compared on how far Black and Hispanic unemployment rates diverged from white unemployment rates over the same period.

Strong GDP growth does not automatically close racial unemployment gaps — but the pattern differs meaningfully between the best and worst performing metro economies.

Methodology

GDP Performance Score

Each MSA is evaluated using a score that balances growth rate against volatility. A metro that grew quickly but erratically scores lower than one with steady, compounding gains.

Score = CAGR ÷ Volatility

CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) is calculated from 2009 to 2023 using real GDP in chained dollars. Volatility is the standard deviation of annual GDP growth rates from 2010 to 2023. This approach helps control for business-cycle bias that disproportionately inflates or deflates scores for smaller MSAs with thinner economic bases.

Unemployment Rate Gaps

Labor market disparity is measured as the difference in unemployment rates between racial and ethnic groups relative to white workers. Four gap measures are tracked separately by race and gender:

gap_mbwBlack minus White — Male
gap_mhwHispanic minus White — Male
gap_fbwBlack minus White — Female
gap_fhwHispanic minus White — Female

A positive gap means the minority group faces a higher unemployment rate than white workers in the same metro area and year. A gap at or near zero indicates near parity.

Data

Three primary data sources were combined for this analysis. GDP data comes from the Bureau of Economic Analysis at the county and MSA level; labor market data comes from the U.S. Census Bureau; and a geographic crosswalk links county FIPS codes to MSA definitions.

FileDescription
Msa_county_reference.xlsx MSA–county FIPS crosswalk used to aggregate county-level GDP to the MSA level
msa_data.csv MSA-level unemployment rate gap data. Source: U.S. Census Bureau
msagdp_raw.csv Real GDP by MSA (LineCode = 2). Source: BEA CAGDP1 — County and Metropolitan Area GDP Summary

All GDP figures are in real (inflation-adjusted) chained dollars. The analysis covers 2010–2023 for unemployment gaps and 2009–2023 for GDP growth, with 2009 serving as the base year for CAGR calculations.

Key Takeaways

1

The CAGR/Volatility scoring method surfaces metros with durable growth rather than those that simply peaked during a particular cycle — an important distinction when studying labor market outcomes over a 14-year window.

2

Black–White unemployment gaps are consistently larger and more persistent than Hispanic–White gaps across both top and bottom performing metros, for both men and women.

3

The 2020 COVID shock appears prominently in the gap series for most metros, with gaps widening sharply before partially recovering — though the speed and completeness of that recovery varies across the distribution.

4

High GDP growth scores do not guarantee narrower racial unemployment gaps, suggesting that labor market equity is shaped by factors beyond aggregate economic performance alone.

Tools & Code

All data processing, analysis, and visualization was completed in SAS 9.4 using PROC SQL, PROC SGPANEL, PROC SGPLOT, and PROC REPORT. The full annotated script is available on GitHub.

Data cleaning steps included reshaping raw BEA GDP files from wide to long format, handling suppressed values (NA, NM), and aligning FIPS-level GDP data to MSA definitions via a county crosswalk before aggregating.
